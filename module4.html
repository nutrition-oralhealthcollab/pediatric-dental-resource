MODULE 04

Feeding Behaviors, Oral Habits & Dental Consequences

Speech-language pathologists are uniquely positioned to identify and address behaviors that drive dental consequences — because those behaviors are often the same ones driving feeding dysfunction.

Tongue Thrust & Malocclusion Indicators

Tongue thrusting affects both swallowing efficiency and dental arch development. A persistent forward tongue position during swallow exerts continuous pressure on the anterior dentition, often resulting in an anterior open bite or flared incisors.

SLP Observation Note

Observe resting posture. Is the tongue positioned against the palate or resting against the lower lip? Check for mouth breathing during non-feeding tasks.

Non-Nutritive Sucking

Prolonged pacifier or digit use alters arch development, leading to posterior crossbites and narrowed maxillary arches.

Clinical Risk Level High Risk

Sensory Oral Aversion: Hygiene Barriers

Oral sensory aversion creates significant barriers to both food acceptance and consistent toothbrushing. Clinicians must address tactile defensiveness to ensure long-term periodontal health.

Bruxism & Motor Organization

Bruxism may reflect the same neurological substrate as feeding motor disorganization. Rhythmic jaw movements outside of mastication may signal a need for increased proprioceptive input or indicate underlying autonomic dysregulation.
